## Figure 1.1: Original vs. Synthetic Data Histograms
**File:** `figures/task1_cleaning/fig_original_vs_synthetic.png`

### 1. What This Figure Shows
Overlay probability-density histograms comparing the distributions of all 16 hydrochemical parameters between the original dataset (n = 45) and the CMGP-augmented synthetic dataset (n = 150). Each subplot presents one parameter with dual-colored semi-transparent bars, enabling direct visual assessment of distributional fidelity.

### 2. Axes & Labels
- **X-axis:** Parameter value in native units (mg/L for most; µS/cm for EC; unitless for pH)
- **Y-axis:** Probability Density (density = True normalization, so total area under each curve = 1.0). This allows fair comparison despite the 45 vs. 150 sample-size asymmetry.

### 3. Key Observations
- pH distributions overlap well (mean diff +2.8%), confirming the synthetic engine preserves the acidic regime (mean ≈ 5.93).
- EC and TDS synthetic distributions closely track the originals (diff −1.6% and −1.2%, respectively).
- NO₃ shows the largest departure (−18.0% mean diff), reflecting the 5-layer noise injection stressing this high-variance parameter.
- Iron and F, having low absolute values and narrow ranges, show near-perfect distributional overlap.
- The multi-modal structure visible in Na and K is faithfully reproduced.

### 4. Statistical Evidence
- KS-test: All 16 parameters PASS (p > 0.05) — no statistically significant distribution shift.
- Average Cohen's d = 0.077 (negligible effect size).
- Inter-parameter correlation preserved at 92.6%.
- Mahalanobis distance = 0.414 (well within the χ² critical threshold).

### 5. Hydrochemical Implication
The CMGP framework successfully augments the dataset without distorting the underlying geochemistry. The synthetic samples inhabit the same chemical space as the originals, ensuring that downstream analyses (compliance, source attribution, ML models) are not biased by artefactual distributions.

### 6. Geogenic vs. Anthropogenic Relevance
The preserved distributions ensure that NO₃ signatures (anthropogenic) and silicate-weathering signatures (Ca/Mg ratios, HCO₃) carry through faithfully to the augmented dataset. No spurious geogenic or anthropogenic signals are introduced.

### 7. Connection to IS 10500:2012
Parameter ranges in the synthetic data remain within the same compliance-violation space as the originals. Iron (mean ≈ 0.30 mg/L, limit 0.3), pH (mean ≈ 5.93, limit 6.5–8.5), and NO₃ (mean ≈ 27.6 mg/L, limit 45) all retain their exceedance profiles.

### 8. Seasonal Context
The synthetic samples are generated conditioned on Season × Area_Type, so each seasonal stratum is independently augmented. This preserves the monsoon dilution and premonsoon concentration patterns visible in the original 45 samples.

### 9. Limitations
- Density normalization can mask absolute frequency differences.
- NO₃ −18% shift is the largest single-parameter drift; resampling or weighted calibration could reduce it.
- Visual comparison is qualitative — the quantitative defense is in the KS/Cohen's/Mahalanobis tests.

### 10. Confidence Level
**High** — 16/16 KS-test PASS, Cohen's d < 0.2 (negligible), spatial correlation > 90%.

### 11. Reproducibility Note
Generated by `hydrochemical_analysis.py` Task 1 with Config.RANDOM_STATE = 42. Identical output given the same input Excel file.

- Monsoon samples shift slightly toward higher Na/(Na+Ca) ratios (silicate weathering enhanced by higher water table).

### 4. Statistical Evidence
Gibbs mechanism classification: Rock-Water Interaction = 100% of samples.

### 5. Hydrochemical Implication
Rock-water interaction dominance means that mineral dissolution (silicate weathering, calcite/dolomite dissolution) is the primary control on groundwater chemistry. This does not exclude anthropogenic inputs — it means the baseline chemical matrix is set by geology, with anthropogenic contaminants (NO₃, Cl, K) superimposed.

### 6. Geogenic vs. Anthropogenic Relevance
Gibbs diagrams identify the **geogenic framework** (rock-water interaction) but cannot distinguish superimposed anthropogenic signals. Complementary tools (ionic ratios, NBL, PIG) are needed for that distinction.

### 8. Seasonal Context

### 9. Limitations
Gibbs diagrams are a first-order screening tool. They identify the dominant mechanism but cannot differentiate specific mineral sources or quantify anthropogenic vs. geogenic proportions.

## A. Geogenic vs. Anthropogenic Summary

### Geogenic Sources
1. **Rock-Water Interaction:** All 45 samples plot in the Rock-Water Interaction zone on Gibbs diagrams (Figure 5.3). This is the dominant mechanism controlling baseline groundwater chemistry.
2. **Silicate Weathering:** Ca/Mg > 2 throughout (Figure 5.4), confirming weathering of silicate minerals (plagioclase, pyroxene) in the charnockite-khondalite terrain.
3. **Iron from Laterite:** PC4 (8.2% variance) is a pure Iron factor (loading 0.793, Figure 5.9), reflecting dissolution of ferruginous minerals in the lateritic cap. 42.2% of samples exceed Iron NBL (0.3 mg/L).
4. **Natural Acidity:** pH < 6.5 in 75.4% of samples (Figure 4.1) — driven by CO₂-charged recharge through organic-rich soil and laterite.
5. **Carbonate Equilibrium:** PC2 (13.6% variance) = HCO₃/Alkalinity axis (Figure 5.7), reflecting natural bicarbonate buffering.

### Anthropogenic Sources
1. **Nitrate Contamination:** 88.9% of samples exceed the NO₃ NBL (10 mg/L). Source: fertilizer leaching, septic tank infiltration, dumping yard leachate. NO₃ loads on PC3 negatively (−0.475), separating it from geogenic parameters.
2. **Chloride Enrichment:** 62.2% of samples exceed Cl NBL (50 mg/L). Postmonsoon Na/Cl drops to 0.51 (Figure 5.4) — excess Cl from waste/sewage.
3. **Potassium from Fertilizers:** K exceeds 12 mg/L (IS 10500 limit) in 28.2% of samples, peaking during monsoon (+121.6%, Figure 3.1) — potash fertilizer wash.
4. **Industrial Input:** IA locations show highest PIG values (e.g., IA-4 Monsoon: PIG = 6.193) and maximum NO₃ (IA-1 Monsoon: 95.2 mg/L).

### Mixed Attribution
- **35/45 samples (77.8%)** classified as Mixed, **10/45 (22.2%)** as Geogenic, **0% as purely Anthropogenic** (Source Analysis Report).
- This means even the "worst" samples retain a geogenic signature (silicate weathering, rock-water interaction), with anthropogenic contamination superimposed.

### 1. **pH Non-Compliance (75.4% Unsafe)**
- **Severity:** Most pervasive violation across all locations and seasons.
- **Mechanism:** Natural acidity from CO₂-charged recharge through lateritic soil and organic matter decomposition.
- **Classification:** Geogenic.
- **Action Required:** Limestone contactors, lime dosing, or aeration for treatment. Cannot be addressed by source protection alone since it is geologically inherent.

### 2. **Iron Contamination (45.1% Unsafe, No Relaxation)**
- **Severity:** Nearly half of all samples exceed the 0.3 mg/L absolute limit (Table 2, No Relaxation).
- **Mechanism:** Dissolution of ferruginous minerals in laterite during monsoon reducing conditions.
- **Classification:** Geogenic (PC4, loading 0.793).
- **Action Required:** Aeration + sand filtration or dedicated iron removal plants. Particularly critical during monsoon (34 exceedances) and postmonsoon (30 exceedances).

### 3. **Nitrate Contamination (88.9% Exceed NBL, 11.8% Exceed IS 10500)**
- **Severity:** 88.9% of samples exceed the natural background level (NBL = 10 mg/L), with 11.8% exceeding the 45 mg/L IS 10500 limit (No Relaxation). Maximum NO₃ = 95.2 mg/L at IA-1 Monsoon.
- **Mechanism:** Fertilizer leaching (K co-exceedance), septic/sewage infiltration, dumping yard leachate.
- **Classification:** Anthropogenic — the most significant anthropogenic contaminant.
- **Action Required:** Source protection zones, regulated fertilizer application, sewage treatment, denitrification units. Postmonsoon monitoring is critical (delayed leaching pattern).

### 4. **Potassium Exceedance (28.2% Unsafe)**
- **Severity:** Nearly one-third of samples exceed the 12 mg/L FSSAI/WHO limit, concentrated during monsoon (+121.6%).
- **Mechanism:** Potash (KCl, K₂SO₄) fertilizer wash during monsoon rainfall.
- **Classification:** Anthropogenic.
- **Action Required:** Controlled fertigation, buffer strips, and agronomic best practices to reduce K runoff.

### 5. **Dissolved Oxygen Depletion (8.7% Unsafe, 35.9% Caution)**
- **Severity:** Combined 44.6% of samples below adequate DO levels (CPCB Class A: 6 mg/L minimum).
- **Mechanism:** Organic pollution, stagnant conditions, microbial oxygen demand — worsened during monsoon (mean DO = 5.02 mg/L, 16 unsafe samples).
- **Classification:** Anthropogenic — organic loading from waste and sewage.
- **Action Required:** Aeration, removal of organic pollution sources, periodic well flushing, exclusion of contamination sources near wells.

## Overall Assessment

The groundwater system of Bhubaneswar, Odisha, is characterized by a **geogenic baseline** (lateritic acidity, iron enrichment, silicate weathering) with **significant anthropogenic overprint** (nitrate, chloride, potassium from agricultural and urban sources). The monsoon season amplifies both geogenic (enhanced dissolution) and anthropogenic (runoff mobilization) contamination, making it the worst period for water quality. Only 6.7% of sample-season combinations are safe for drinking per IS 10500:2012.
